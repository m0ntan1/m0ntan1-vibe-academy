M0NTAN1 FINANCIAL: Building an Autonomous Trading Firm with AI

📅 Started March 12, 2026 🏗 13 Agents · 5 Departments 🛠 Python · Alpaca · Discord · SQLite

One person. One question: can you build an autonomous trading firm with AI? Not a trading bot. Not a weekend hack. A simulated financial firm where every named role maps to a real software function, and the org chart IS the system architecture. This is the story of building it, breaking it, and learning what the documentation never tells you.

🏔
Montani Semper Liberi

Mountaineers are always free. This project was built in West Virginia, by a West Virginian, proving that world-class technical work doesn't require a Silicon Valley zip code. The mountains teach patience. The markets demand it.

Section 1 of 7

The Idea

March 12, 2026. The premise was simple and maybe a little reckless: build an autonomous trading firm where AI agents fill every role, from CEO to trader to risk officer. Not a script that buys when RSI drops below 30. A functioning organization with a chain of command, specialized departments, internal disagreements, and a kill switch.

The difference matters. A trading bot is one strategy, one loop, one failure mode. A trading firm has research analysts who watch the macro environment, risk officers who can override traders, and a market intelligence department that knows the difference between a Tuesday morning dip and a systemic crisis. The firm survives what the bot doesn't.

Section 2 of 7

The Research

Before writing a single line of code, we studied 10 documented cases of trading bot failures. Not theoretical failures. Real systems that lost real money (or paper money in revealing ways). The patterns were clear and brutal.

The Silent Differentiator: Event Awareness

The single biggest finding across all 10 cases: bots that ignore economic calendars, earnings dates, and macro events consistently blow up. It doesn't matter how good your signal is. If your momentum bot buys TSLA the morning before an FOMC rate decision, the signal is noise. Event awareness is not a feature. It's a survival requirement.

⚠️
Key Research Finding

Every failed bot we studied shared one trait: it operated as if the market existed in a vacuum. No awareness of what was happening outside the price chart. The market is not a math problem. It's a complex system driven by human decisions, government policy, and global events.

1

Mean reversion fails without regime awareness (Case 1)

A bot buying "cheap" stocks in a crashing market is not finding value. It's catching knives. Mean reversion requires the ability to identify the current market regime and know when the strategy is invalid.

2

DCA outperforms timing in volatile conditions (Cases 2-3)

Dollar-cost averaging is boring. It also survived scenarios that destroyed timing-based strategies. When volatility spikes, the simple approach wins because it doesn't need to be right about direction on any given day.

3

Momentum thrives in trends, dies in sideways and crisis (Cases 6-7)

Momentum strategies printed money in clear trends and hemorrhaged it in sideways chop and sudden crises. The strategy isn't broken. Using it in the wrong regime is broken.

4

Multi-agent architecture proved superior (Case 10)

The TradingAgents paper showed specialized agents outperforming monolithic bots. Different roles bring different perspectives. Disagreement between agents creates robustness. This became the foundation of the entire M0NTAN1 Financial architecture.

✅
The Takeaway

No strategy works all the time. Every strategy works some of the time. The winning architecture doesn't pick the best strategy. It picks the right strategy for the current conditions and has the discipline to sit out when no strategy fits.

Section 3 of 7

The Plan

The research produced 12 immutable design principles. These aren't aspirational guidelines. They're load-bearing constraints that shaped every technical decision in the system.

01

The metaphor IS the architecture

Every org chart role maps to a real software function. CEO makes portfolio-level decisions. Risk officer evaluates exposure. Traders execute strategies. The metaphor isn't for documentation. It IS the code structure.

02

Chain of command prevents emotional override

A trader can't override the risk officer. The risk officer can't override the CEO's strategic direction. Hierarchy exists because in volatile markets, you need something more disciplined than "the last agent to run wins."

03

No firing. Retrain via transfer learning.

When an agent underperforms, it doesn't get deleted. It gets retrained. Its historical data is preserved, its parameters adjusted, its strategy evolved. Destroying knowledge is never the answer.

04

Agents must disagree

Consensus is dangerous. If all three traders want the same position, the system should be suspicious, not confident. Built-in disagreement creates the tension that prevents groupthink blowups.

05

Data contract before code

Before any agent writes a report, the schema is agreed upon. Standardized formats. Known fields. Predictable structures. If you can't define the output, you can't build the consumer.

06

HR files = configuration specs

Each agent has an HR file. The backstory encodes trading philosophy. The personality section governs parameter selection. "Cautious and methodical" isn't flavor text. It maps to specific risk tolerance values.

07

Knowledge is never deleted

Every trade, every decision, every market observation is stored permanently. Bad trades are as valuable as good ones. The system's memory is its competitive advantage.

08

Separated risk as architecture

The risk management department has no trading incentive. It exists only to evaluate, constrain, and protect. A risk officer who also trades has a conflict of interest baked into the code.

09

Event awareness as defense

The market analyst's primary job is preventing blind-spot blowups. Before any trade executes, the system checks: what's happening today? Earnings? FOMC? GDP release? No trading in ignorance.

10

Regime-conditional activation

Agents only trade in their regime. The momentum trader sits out sideways markets. The mean reversion trader sits out crashes. Knowing when NOT to trade is the hardest lesson, so it's enforced by architecture.

11

Delta tracking is core

Every collector must track what changed since its last sweep. Not "what is the current state" but "what moved." Deltas compound over time. They're the raw material for pattern recognition.

12

Gated phase progression

Phases advance based on criteria, not calendars. Phase 1 doesn't end after 30 days. It ends when specific technical milestones are proven. Time pressure is the enemy of quality in software and in markets.

The Shark Base Kill Switch

🦈
Shark Base Protocol

When portfolio drawdown exceeds a defined threshold, the system enters Shark Base: all trading halts, all open orders cancel, and the system enters a mandatory cool-down period. No agent can override it. No parameter tweak can bypass it. The kill switch exists because the most dangerous moment in trading is when you're losing and convinced you can make it back.

The Build

The framework is Python, running against Alpaca's paper trading API with $100,000 in simulated equity. The choice was deliberate: Alpaca provides real market data, real order execution semantics, and real broker constraints without risking real capital during development.

Jett Reno

Momentum Trader

Rides trends. Buys breakouts, sells breakdowns. Active in trending regimes, dormant in sideways chop. Named for a character who fixes things under pressure.

Silas Grey

DCA Strategist

Dollar-cost averages into positions over time. The patient one. Doesn't care about daily noise. Accumulates during volatility when other agents are sidelined.

Vance Moody

Mean Reversion

Buys oversold, sells overbought. Only active when regime analysis confirms a ranging market. The most regime-dependent agent on the desk.

Bosephus

Operations / Order Execution

Every trade flows through Bosephus. Pre-flight validation, position sizing, order routing. The gatekeeper between strategy and execution.

Risk Management: Wiltmire's 3-Layer Model

1

Pre-Trade Risk

Before any order submits, Wiltmire evaluates: position size relative to portfolio, correlation with existing holdings, sector concentration, and current market regime. A trade can be rejected before it ever reaches the broker.

2

Portfolio-Level Risk

Continuous monitoring of aggregate exposure, drawdown from peak, sector/asset concentration, and correlation risk. The portfolio view catches what individual trade analysis misses.

3

Regime-Conditional Limits

Risk parameters shift based on market regime. In a crisis regime, position sizes shrink, stop-losses tighten, and new entries require higher conviction thresholds. The system gets more conservative exactly when it needs to.

Section 5 of 7

Everyone Has a Plan Until They Get Punched in the Mouth

This is the heart of the case study. The plan was solid. The architecture was sound. Then the system met the real world and the real world punched back. Here's every hit, what broke, and how it got fixed. These are the lessons you won't find in any API documentation or trading tutorial.

🏔
Mountain Wisdom

In West Virginia, we have a saying: the road to the top is never straight. Every switchback below is something that looked like a straight path on the map but turned out to be a cliff face. You adapt or you don't make it.

Impact 1

Bracket orders only support day time-in-force

The assumption: submit a bracket order (entry + stop loss + take profit) and let it ride for days. The reality: Alpaca bracket orders only accept day TIF. All three legs expire at market close. Every single day. A multi-day swing trade with bracket risk management? Doesn't exist.

Fix: Built manual leg management. The system tracks each leg independently, re-submits protective orders each morning, and handles partial fills gracefully. More code, more edge cases, but now the system actually works across multiple days.

Impact 2

Pre-market data limitation

The plan: scan pre-market movers starting at 4 AM to position before the opening bell. The reality: Alpaca's free tier doesn't provide pre-market quote data. The morning momentum scan can't start until 9:30 AM when the regular session opens.

Fix: Redesigned the morning routine to use alternative data sources for pre-market awareness and shifted the momentum scan to react to opening-bell price action rather than pre-market signals. Not ideal, but functional.

Impact 3

Extended hours vs. order type conflict

Extended hours trading on Alpaca only works with pure limit orders. You cannot combine the extended hours flag with stop or stop_limit order types. You also can't have both GTC persistence and extended hours access on the same order. The result: complex daily operational overhead to re-place orders each morning.

Fix: Accepted the constraint. Built a morning re-initialization routine that evaluates overnight price changes, re-calculates stop levels, and re-submits all protective orders for the new trading day. The overhead is real but the discipline it forces is actually valuable.

Impact 4

Database/broker position drift

The assumption: the database always reflects the broker's actual positions. The reality: drift happens from network timeouts, partial fills, manual broker-side operations, and edge cases in order lifecycle events. Your database says you own 50 shares of AAPL. The broker says 47.

Fix: Built portfolio reconciliation as Step 0.5, running before every trading cycle. The system pulls actual broker positions, compares them against the database, flags discrepancies, and auto-corrects when the delta is within tolerance. Larger deltas require human review.

Impact 5

Sell orders creating accidental short positions

A sell order based on stale database quantity could overshoot the actual position and accidentally open a short. The database says you have 100 shares. You send a sell for 100. But the broker only has 95 (see Impact 4). Now you're short 5 shares you never intended to hold.

Fix: Built multi-layer pre-flight validation in Bosephus. Before any sell order submits, the system: (a) checks the database quantity, (b) queries the broker for the actual quantity, (c) uses the LOWER of the two, and (d) rejects the order entirely if the discrepancy exceeds a threshold.

Impact 6

Market context contamination

Weekend crypto trading was contaminating performance data. An ETH trade placed on Saturday during crypto-only hours would get mixed into the same analysis as equity trades placed during full market sessions. The performance metrics were lying because they blended fundamentally different market conditions.

Fix: Built a complete market context tagging system. Every data write across all 7 affected tables gets tagged with one of four contexts: FULL_MARKET, EXTENDED_EQUITY, CRYPTO_ONLY, or MARKET_HOLIDAY. Analysis filters by context. Weekend crypto gains never inflate weekday equity performance again.

Impact 7

TSLA breakout entry failed on Days 1-2

Jett Reno identified a TSLA breakout setup and placed a day-TIF stop entry order. The price didn't reach the trigger level that day. Order expired. Same thing the next day. By Day 3, the breakout happened but the order wasn't active.

Fix: Changed breakout entry orders to GTC (good-till-cancelled) stops. Breakout strategies require patience. The signal might be right. The timing might take days. A day-TIF entry on a breakout is structurally incompatible with how breakouts work.

Impact 8

Voice pipeline blocked

The plan included voice-generated briefings through ElevenLabs. The free tier blocks API voice generation entirely. No degraded service. No rate limit. Just blocked.

Fix: Parked for future evaluation. This was a nice-to-have feature, not a core dependency. Text-based Discord reports work fine. When the system generates enough value to justify the subscription, the voice pipeline can be re-enabled.

Reflection

What these failures teach

Notice the pattern. Every single failure came from an assumption that felt reasonable but was never tested against the actual API, the actual broker, or the actual market. The code was fine. The architecture was fine. The assumptions were wrong.

  • Read the constraints page first. Not the getting-started guide. The constraints page. The limitations docs. The "known issues" list. That's where reality lives.
  • Test with real order types on Day 1. Don't build a trade execution engine and test order types on Day 5. Submit a bracket order. See what happens. Learn the constraints before you build around assumptions.
  • Assume drift. Any system that talks to an external service over a network will drift. Build reconciliation from the start, not as a patch.
  • Tag everything. If your data can originate in different contexts, tag the context at write time. Not later. Not in a migration. At write time.

Advanced Techniques

Beyond the core architecture and the hard lessons, several techniques emerged during the build that are worth examining on their own. These aren't hacks or workarounds. They're design patterns that apply far beyond trading systems.

Market Context Tagging

Every single data write in the system carries a market context tag. This isn't optional. The system determines the current market state (FULL_MARKET during regular equity hours, EXTENDED_EQUITY during pre/post market, CRYPTO_ONLY on weekends, MARKET_HOLIDAY on market holidays) and stamps it on the record. Queries can then filter by context, preventing cross-contamination of performance metrics.

The Delta Engine Philosophy

Every data collector in the system tracks what changed since its last sweep. Not "what is the current state" but "what moved, and by how much." This is the delta engine. Raw prices are useful. Knowing that AAPL moved +2.3% since the last check while SPY moved -0.5% is actionable intelligence.

Deltas compound over time. A stock that moves +0.5% per check for 8 consecutive checks is telling you something different from a stock that moved +4% in one check and went flat. The delta history IS the signal. Without it, you're looking at snapshots. With it, you're watching a film.

✅
Design Pattern

Delta tracking applies to any system that monitors changing state: inventory management, infrastructure monitoring, social media analytics, sensor networks. If it changes over time, track the deltas, not just the current value.

Regime-Conditional Agent Activation

This is not "turn agents on and off." It's a matrix of market conditions crossed with agent capabilities. Each agent declares which regimes it operates in. The market intelligence department classifies the current regime. The system activates only the agents whose declared regimes match.

Separated Risk as Architecture

In most trading bots, risk management is a function inside the trading logic. "If position size exceeds X, reduce." This couples risk evaluation to trading incentive. The agent that wants to trade is also the agent deciding if the trade is too risky. That's a fox guarding the henhouse.

In M0NTAN1 Financial, risk management is a separate department with its own agents, its own data, and its own authority. Wiltmire (Chief Risk Officer) can reject a trade that every trader wants. He has no bonus tied to trade volume. His only metric is drawdown prevention.

HR Files as Configuration Specs

Each agent has a detailed HR file that reads like a personnel record. This isn't creative writing exercise. The backstory section encodes trading philosophy that maps to strategy selection. The personality traits map to parameter values. "Aggressive and conviction-driven" produces different position sizing math than "cautious and evidence-based."

New team members (human or AI) can read an HR file and understand what an agent does, why it does it, and how it will behave in different conditions, without reading a line of code. The documentation is the configuration.

Data Contract Before Code

Before any new report or data pipeline was built, the producing agent and all consuming agents agreed on a schema. Field names. Data types. Update frequency. Null handling. This contract was finalized before implementation started. When Declan's regime classification feeds into Jett Reno's activation logic, both sides agree on what "trending" means, what confidence threshold triggers it, and what the fallback is when classification fails.

💡
Why This Matters Beyond Trading

Every technique in this section applies to any multi-component system. Microservices need data contracts. Monitoring systems need delta tracking. Role-based access systems need separated concerns. Trading just makes the consequences of getting it wrong extremely visible and extremely fast.

Final Thought

The real lesson of M0NTAN1 Financial

This project is not about building a profitable trading bot. It might become that. But the real lesson is about systems thinking. About building architectures that survive contact with reality. About the difference between a plan and an operational system. About the 8 things that broke and the discipline to fix each one properly instead of hacking around them.

If you're a student in MØNTAN1 Academy, study this case not for the trading knowledge. Study it for the engineering patterns: separated concerns, data contracts, delta tracking, reconciliation as a first-class feature, and the humility to assume your assumptions are wrong until proven otherwise.

  • The metaphor IS the architecture. Pick a metaphor that maps to your problem domain and commit to it.
  • Test constraints before building features. One day of API constraint testing saves a week of refactoring.
  • Tag everything at write time. Context that isn't captured when data is created is context that's gone forever.
  • Build reconciliation from Day 1. Any system talking to external services will drift. Plan for it.
  • Disagreement is a feature. Systems that always agree with themselves are fragile. Build in tension.
